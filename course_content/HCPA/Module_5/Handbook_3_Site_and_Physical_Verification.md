# MODULE 5: Property Verification & Due Diligence

## Handbook 3: Site & Physical Verification

*"Never buy property you have not walked on."*

### Opening Story
An investor in the United States purchased four plots of land in a branded estate in Epe. He trusted the videos sent by the developer showing a beautiful gatehouse and cleared land. The developer assured him his plots were "ready for immediate construction."

Two years later, the investor visited Nigeria and asked to see his plots. 

To his horror, he found that his allocated plots were situated directly inside a deep valley that accumulated runoff water from the surrounding area. The soil was loose mud, and the depth of the water was over 4 feet during the rainy season. To build on this land, he would need to spend over ₦15 million per plot on sand-filling and piling before starting a foundation. 

The developer had sold him swampy land at dry-land prices.

This loss could have been prevented by a physical site inspection and a soil topography check.

### Learning Objectives
By the end of this handbook, you should be able to:
- Conduct a professional physical inspection of land and buildings.
- Pick accurate GPS coordinates using handheld devices or mobile applications.
- Identify common physical and geographical red flags on a property.
- Determine setback requirements for power lines, pipelines, and roads.

### Lesson 1: The Physical Inspection Protocol

A physical site inspection is not just a casual walk. It is a systematic audit of the land and its surroundings. You must walk the land and document its physical characteristics:

#### 1. Boundary Audit & Beacon Matching
Every registered plot has concrete boundary markers called **beacons**, each stamped with a unique identification number. You must physically locate these beacons at the corners of the plot and verify that their positions and numbers match the survey plan. If beacons are missing or do not match, the plot boundary is subject to dispute.

#### 2. Topographical & Soil Analysis
- **Topography:** Is the land flat, sloped, or situated in a depression? Land located below the road level will collect rainwater from the surrounding streets. This requires expensive sand-filling (which can add millions of Naira to construction costs).
- **Soil Composition:** Is the soil sandy, clay-based, or organic peat? Sandy soil has excellent load-bearing capacity and is cheap to build on. Clay or peat requires deep excavation, raft foundations, or piling—which can double your construction budget. Look for water-loving plants (like bamboo clusters or water hyacinths), which indicate a high water table and marshy conditions.

#### 3. Infrastructure & Utility Access
- **Road Access:** Is there a dedicated road to the plot? Is the road paved, graded, or a dirt track? If there is no road access, you might need to buy access rights from neighbors.
- **Utility Proximity:** How far is the nearest electricity pole? Is there a public drainage system? Building in an area without drainage will lead to seasonal flooding.

### Lesson 2: How to Pick GPS Coordinates

You must never rely on coordinates written on a survey plan without verifying them on the ground. Dishonest sellers sometimes print fake coordinates on surveys to make government acquisition land appear free.

#### Steps to Pick Coordinates:
1. **Stand on the Land:** Walk to the center or the boundary beacon pillars of the plot.
2. **Use a GPS Device or Mobile App:** Use a dedicated GPS receiver or an app like *GPS Essentials* or *Land Calculator* on a smartphone.
3. **Wait for High Accuracy:** Let the device connect to multiple satellites until the accuracy reading is under 3 meters (10 feet).
4. **Record the Coordinates:** Note down the Easting and Northing values (UTM format) or Latitude and Longitude.
5. **Cross-Reference:** Compare the ground coordinates with the coordinates written on the seller's survey plan. If they differ by more than 5 meters, the survey plan is suspicious.

### Lesson 3: Top Physical Red Flags

When inspecting a site, look out for these warning signs:

#### 1. Setback Encroachments
- **High-Tension Power Lines:** Under Nigerian electrical safety regulations, high-tension lines require a legal setback of 15 to 30 meters. Building within this setback is highly dangerous (due to electrocution and radiation risks) and illegal; the government will eventually demolish the structure.
- **NNPC Oil/Gas Pipelines:** Require a minimum setback of 25 to 50 meters. Look for yellow concrete warning posts or cleared pipeline corridors.
- **Drainage Canals:** Require a setback of 10 to 15 meters to allow maintenance access and prevent flooding.

#### 2. Visual Markings & Disputes
- Look for red spray-painted messages on walls: *"This land is not for sale"*, *"Caveat Emptor"* (Buyer Beware), or *"Demolition Order / LASG"* (Lagos State Government).
- Check for signs of active dispute: broken fences, multiple security guards from different factions, or warnings from neighbors.

#### 3. Topographical Hazards
- Soil erosion channels, sinkholes, or swampy vegetation (e.g., bamboo tree clusters or water hyacinths, which indicate permanent marshland).

### Case Study: The Pipeline Trap

> [!NOTE]
> **Scenario:** Mr. Yusuf wanted to buy a cheap commercial plot along a major road in Ogun State. The price was 50% below the market average. The seller claimed the discount was due to "urgent medical bills."
> 
> A Housmata Advisor went to inspect the site. While walking the land, the advisor noticed a concrete marker post partially hidden in the grass with the letters **"NNPC"** (Nigerian National Petroleum Corporation) painted on it.
> 
> **The Analysis:** The advisor walked the perimeter and confirmed that a major national petroleum pipeline ran directly under the middle of the plot. The entire land fell within the pipeline setback zone, meaning no permanent structure could ever be built there.
> 
> **Outcome:** The transaction was aborted. The seller knew about the pipeline and was trying to dump the useless land on an unsuspecting buyer.
> 
> **Lesson:** Always look for physical markers, concrete posts, and indicators of underground utilities during site inspections.

---

### Chapter Summary
- Physical verification confirms that the land matches the paper records and is physically buildable.
- Advisors must pick coordinates directly from boundary pillars to prevent coordinate fraud.
- Physical red flags include power line setbacks, pipeline corridors, swamp vegetation, and dispute markings.
- Land topography significantly impacts future construction costs (sand-filling, drainage, piling).
