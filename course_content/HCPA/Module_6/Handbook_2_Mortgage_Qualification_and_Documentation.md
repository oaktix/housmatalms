# MODULE 6: Mortgage Readiness & Home Financing

## Handbook 2: Mortgage Qualification & Documentation

*"Verification of income is the key that unlocks the bank vault."*

### Opening Story
A successful fashion designer in Lagos, who owned a boutique in Lekki, applied for a mortgage to buy a townhome. Her business generated over ₦5 million in cash sales monthly. She had ₦40 million in her personal savings account, ready for her equity contribution.

However, she did not keep audited financial records, she mixed her personal expenses with business revenues, and she paid her staff in cash. Her corporate tax filings were incomplete.

Despite her high actual income, the mortgage bank rejected her application within days. The bank credit officer stated: *"We cannot verify your stable net personal income from your bank statements, and your business lacks audited accounts."*

She was wealthy, but she was not **mortgage ready**.

### Lesson 1: How Lenders Assess Borrowers

Lenders evaluate mortgage applicants using the **5 Cs of Credit**. As a Property Advisor, you must analyze your client's profile against these criteria:

1. **Character (Credit History):** Does the borrower pay bills on time? Lenders check this by running searches on national credit bureaus (e.g., CRC Credit Bureau, FirstCentral). They look at past defaults, outstanding loans, and payment consistencies. A single active default will lead to immediate rejection.
2. **Capacity (Repayment Ability):** Does the borrower have sufficient, stable income to cover the monthly repayments after meeting other debt obligations? Capacity is evaluated using bank statements and tax filings.
3. **Capital (Equity):** How much money has the borrower saved for their equity contribution? Lenders prefer borrowers who have "skin in the game" because they are less likely to walk away from the property during a downturn.
4. **Collateral (The Property):** The property itself is the security. The bank's legal team will verify that the title is clean (C of O or Governor's Consent) and run an independent valuation to ensure the property is worth the loan amount.
5. **Conditions:** The terms of the loan (interest rates, tenure) and the overall economic environment (inflation, job stability in the borrower's industry).

### Lesson 2: Qualification by Profile

Lenders require different documentation based on the borrower's source of income:

#### 1. Salary Earners (Verifiable Employees)
This is the easiest profile for lenders to approve because of income predictability.
- **Key Requirements:** The applicant must be a confirmed employee of a reputable company, typically with a minimum of 2 years of work history in the same industry.
- **Required Documents:**
  - Letter of Employment & Confirmation of Appointment.
  - Pay slips for the last 3 to 6 months.
  - Personal salary bank statements for the last 6 months (matching the pay slips).
  - Tax Clearance Certificate (TCC) or proof of PAYE tax deductions.
  - Pension statement printout.

#### 2. Business Owners & Self-Employed (Entrepreneurs)
Lenders are cautious with self-employed applicants because their business revenue fluctuates.
- **Key Requirements:** The business must have been registered and active for a minimum of 3 to 5 years, showing consistent corporate profitability.
- **Required Documents:**
  - Certificate of Incorporation (CAC) and Status Report.
  - Memorandum and Articles of Association.
  - Audited Financial Statements for the last 2 to 3 years (signed by a chartered accountant).
  - Corporate bank statements for the last 12 months.
  - Company Tax Clearance Certificate.
  - Board resolution authorizing the mortgage loan.

#### 3. Diaspora Applicants (Nigerians Living Abroad)
Lenders value diaspora applicants because they earn in strong foreign currencies (USD, GBP, CAD, EUR) that hedge against local currency depreciation.
- **Key Requirements:** Verifiable employment and a clean credit record in their resident country.
- **Required Documents:**
  - Foreign Credit Bureau Report (e.g., Equifax, Experian, TransUnion).
  - Foreign bank statements for the last 6 to 12 months.
  - Employment contract and pay slips.
  - Tax returns filed in their resident country (e.g., W2 in the US, T4 in Canada).
  - Valid Nigerian International Passport.

### Lesson 3: Credit bureau Checks & Credit Assessment

When an application is submitted, the bank credit officer does not just look at your bank balance. They run an audit on national credit databases.

- **The Credit Bureau Report:** Details every loan you have ever taken in Nigeria, including credit card balances, assets pledged, and repayment history. If you have an outstanding debt with a microfinance bank or a telecommunications company, it will show up.
- **Clean-up Strategy:** Advise clients to obtain their own credit reports (available online for a small fee) before applying. If there are errors or old settled debts still marked as active defaults, they must contact the bureau to resolve them before submitting the mortgage application.

### Case Study: Structuring the Entrepreneur's Files

> [!NOTE]
> **Scenario:** Mr. Emeka ran a logistics business that netted ₦3 million monthly. He wanted to buy a home using a commercial mortgage. His bank statements showed high turnover, but he paid himself no salary and withdrew business cash for personal holidays.
> 
> He contacted a Housmata Advisor. The advisor looked at his papers and told him: *"We cannot submit this today. The bank will reject it because you look like a high-risk sole proprietor with no structure."*
> 
> **The Restructuring Plan:** The advisor worked with Mr. Emeka's accountant to implement a 6-month restructuring plan:
> 1. Mr. Emeka was formally registered as an employee of his company.
> 2. The company paid him a fixed salary of ₦800,000 monthly into a dedicated personal salary account.
> 3. He stopped withdrawing business funds directly and instead relied on his salary.
> 4. His accountant prepared audited accounts for the previous two years.
> 
> **Outcome:** After six months of clean, structured salary bank statements and audited corporate records, the mortgage was submitted and approved.
> 
> **Lesson:** Time spent restructuring a client's financial profile before applying is the difference between approval and rejection.

### Chapter Summary
- Lenders use credit bureaus and bank statements to assess character and capacity.
- Salary earners, business owners, and diaspora applicants have distinct documentation pipelines.
- Entrepreneurs must separate personal and business expenses to qualify.
- Property Advisors must audit client documents for inconsistencies and credit red flags before lender submission.
