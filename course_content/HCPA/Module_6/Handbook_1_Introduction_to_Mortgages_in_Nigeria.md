### Lesson 1: How Mortgages Work in Nigeria

Many Nigerians believe that real estate can only be purchased with 100% cash. While cash transactions are common, they limit the market. A **mortgage** is a long-term loan used to purchase property, where the property itself serves as collateral.

#### The Financial Mechanics of Home Leverage:
Instead of saving 100% of the property value (which exposes your money to inflation and currency depreciation over years), a mortgage allows you to purchase the property immediately by paying a fraction of the cost—called the **Equity Contribution**—and borrowing the balance from a lender. The lender holds the title documents as collateral until the loan, plus interest, is fully repaid. This allows you to capture property appreciation early and live in your home while paying off the balance over time.

#### Core Mortgage Terminology:
1. **Equity Contribution (Down Payment):** The percentage of the property purchase price the buyer must pay from their own funds. In Nigeria, this typically ranges from 20% to 40%.
2. **Loan-to-Value (LTV) Ratio:** The percentage of the property value that the lender is willing to finance. If the LTV is 70%, the bank will finance 70%, and the buyer must provide 30% equity.
3. **Interest Rate:** The annual cost of borrowing. In Nigeria, commercial rates are high, typically ranging from 18% to 28%+ per annum (variable rates), while government-backed schemes (NHF) are fixed at 6%.
4. **Tenure:** The lifespan of the loan. Typically ranges from 5 to 20 years in Nigeria, depending on the buyer's age and income stability.

### Lesson 2: NHF vs. Commercial Mortgages

There are two primary mortgage pathways in Nigeria, and you must know how to guide your client to select the right one:

#### 1. National Housing Fund (NHF)
The NHF is a government-backed scheme managed by the Federal Mortgage Bank of Nigeria (FMBN). It is funded by mandatory monthly deductions of 2.5% of the basic salary of Nigerian workers.
- **Pros:** It offers the lowest interest rate in the country: **6% per annum (fixed)**.
- **Cons:** The maximum loan limit is capped at ₦50 million. The processing time is notoriously slow (often taking 6 to 18 months), and the property must meet strict NHF construction and title standards.

#### 2. Commercial Mortgages
These are loans issued by commercial banks (e.g., Access Bank, GTBank) and Primary Mortgage Institutions (PMIs).
- **Pros:** Faster processing time (typically 4 to 8 weeks) and higher loan limits based on your repayment capacity.
- **Cons:** Very high interest rates (18% to 28%+), which significantly increase the total cost of the property over the life of the loan.

| Feature | National Housing Fund (NHF) | Commercial Mortgages |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Funding Source** | FMBN via monthly salary deductions. | Commercial banks and PMIs. |
| **Interest Rate** | 6% per annum (Fixed). | 18% - 28%+ per annum (Variable). |
| **Maximum Loan Limit** | ₦50 million. | Varies based on the borrower's repayment capacity. |
| **Processing Time** | Slow (6 to 18 months). | Faster (4 to 8 weeks). |
| **Property Eligibility** | Must be NHF-approved or have registered title. | Any property with a clean registered title. |

### Lesson 3: The Advisor's Role: Guide, Not Lender

A Housmata Property Advisor is **not** a financial lender. You do not approve loans, issue interest rates, or collect mortgage payments. 

Your role is to act as a **Mortgage Readiness Guide**:
- Educate the client on financing options.
- Help the client calculate their affordability before they apply.
- Guide the client to organize their documentation so they don't get rejected.
- Introduce the client to verified Housmata Finance Partners.

#### Housmata Finance Partners:
Housmata partners with forward-thinking housing finance institutions and fintech platforms—such as **ULE Homes** and **Yalo**—to simplify the home financing process for our clients. These partnerships allow Housmata Advisors to direct pre-qualified clients to institutions that offer faster processing and specialized home financing models.

### Case Study: The Advisor as a Pre-Qualifier

> [!NOTE]
> **Scenario:** Mrs. Amadi wanted to buy a duplex worth ₦100 million. She had ₦30 million in cash. She walked into a commercial bank and applied for a ₦70 million mortgage. The bank rejected her application within one week. Discouraged, she assumed she could never buy the house.
> 
> She contacted a Housmata Advisor, who audited her financials. The advisor discovered that Mrs. Amadi's annual net income was ₦12 million. A ₦70 million mortgage at a 22% interest rate required monthly repayments of approximately ₦1.4 million (₦16.8 million annually)—which exceeded her total annual income.
> 
> **The Advisor's Strategy:** The advisor recalculated her affordability. He recommended a smaller property worth ₦60 million. With her ₦30 million cash as a 50% equity contribution, she needed a loan of only ₦30 million. Her monthly repayment dropped to ₦600,000, which fit her income profile.
> 
> **Outcome:** The bank approved the ₦30 million mortgage, and Mrs. Amadi became a homeowner.
> 
> **Lesson:** Pre-qualifying clients prevents them from applying for loans they cannot afford, saving time and preventing rejection.

### Chapter Summary
- Mortgages are viable financing options in Nigeria for clients with stable, verifiable incomes.
- The National Housing Fund (NHF) offers the lowest interest rates (6%) but has a cap of ₦50 million and takes longer to process.
- Property Advisors do not lend money; they prepare, qualify, and guide clients to partner institutions like ULE Homes and Yalo.
