# MODULE 4: Land Banking & Investment Advisory

## Handbook 4: Exit Strategies & Capstone Project

*"An investment without an exit strategy is just a gamble."*

### Opening Story
Two business partners purchased a 1-hectare (15 plots) tract of land in Epe in 2017 for ₦10 million. They had different visions for the exit.

*Partner A* wanted to wait for the land to appreciate, sell it as a single piece, and take the profit. 

*Partner B* proposed a different path. In 2022, when Epe had developed, they cleared the land, built a gatehouse, graded internal roads, and divided the land into 15 individual residential plots. They branded the project as "Willow Heights Estate" and sold each plot for ₦6 million.

Total sales revenue reached ₦90 million. After spending ₦15 million on basic clearing, fencing, and layouts, their net return was ₦75 million on a ₦10 million investment. Partner A's plan of selling the raw hectare would have fetched only ₦30 million at the time.

Subdividing and branding the exit unlocked 2.5 times more value.

### Lesson 1: The Four Exit Strategies

Buying land is only half the battle. Your client must know how they intend to get their money out with profit before they buy. As an advisor, you should guide them through these four exit models:

#### 1. The Raw Land Exit (The Long Hold)
- **The Concept:** Buy raw, undeveloped land, do nothing to it, and sell it as raw land years later when urban growth reaches the boundary.
- **How it works:** This is a passive strategy. The investor simply holds the title and waits. When developers or builders arrive in the area, they purchase the raw land to build estates.
- **Pros:** Lowest operational effort. No construction risk, no development management, and zero development capital needed.
- **Cons:** Lower profit margins compared to active exits. You sell at wholesale rates rather than retail rates.

#### 2. The Subdivision Exit (Parceling)
- **The Concept:** Buy land in bulk (acres or hectares, which are cheaper per square meter), create a layout plan, clear the land, add basic infrastructure (gatehouse, perimeter fencing), and sell it as individual plots.
- **How it works:** You buy wholesale and sell retail. By dividing a single hectare (15 plots) into individual residential plots, you open the inventory to middle-class buyers who cannot afford a whole hectare but are willing to pay a premium for a single plot.
- **Pros:** Extremely high profit margins. You capture the developer's premium.
- **Cons:** Requires additional development capital, layout design approvals from physical planning ministries, and local logistics coordination.

#### 3. The Joint Venture Exit (JV)
- **The Concept:** The land banker partners with a real estate developer. The land banker contributes the land, and the developer contributes the construction capital. Upon completion of the estate, they share the finished units (duplexes, apartments) or sales revenue based on an agreed ratio.
- **How it works:** This is the preferred strategy for investors holding premium land in high-density urban areas (like Lekki, Ikoyi, or central Abuja). The landowner does not need construction expertise; they leverage the developer's execution power.
- **Pros:** High returns. The landowner transitions from holding raw soil to owning premium apartments or commercial spaces without spending construction capital.
- **Cons:** High risk if the developer defaults, goes bankrupt, or delivers poor quality. Requires watertight legal agreements.

#### 4. The Outright Development Exit (Build to Lease/Sell)
- **The Concept:** The land banker transitions into a developer and constructs residential estates, commercial plazas, or student hostels themselves.
- **Pros:** Captures 100% of the value chain.
- **Cons:** Extremely capital intensive, carries high execution risk, and requires project management expertise.

### Lesson 2: Structure of a Joint Venture (JV)

For landowners holding property in prime locations, joint ventures offer a highly profitable exit. However, they must be structured correctly to prevent legal disputes:

#### 1. Valuation
Independent, registered estate surveyors must value the land. This establishes the landowner's equity contribution. For example, if the land is valued at ₦200 million and the total estimated cost to build the proposed apartments is ₦300 million, the land represents 40% of the total project value.

#### 2. The Sharing Ratio
The sharing ratio is calculated based on the land value versus construction costs. Using the example above, the sharing ratio would be structured as:
- **Landowner:** 40% of the finished apartments or sales revenue.
- **Developer:** 60% of the finished apartments or sales revenue.

#### 3. The Premium
Developers often pay the landowner a non-refundable cash "premium" (typically 5% to 10% of the land value) upon signing the JV agreement. This premium covers the landowner's opportunity cost while the land is tied up during construction.

#### 4. Legal Protections (The Deed of Joint Venture)
A property lawyer must draft a **Deed of Joint Venture** which specifies:
- The exact project timeline (e.g., completion within 24 months).
- Penalty clauses for developer delays.
- A clause retaining the land title in custody or escrow until construction milestones are met.

### Lesson 3: The Investment Advisory Report

Your ability to present land banking opportunities professionally defines your standing as a top-tier Property Advisor. You must learn to write an **Investment Advisory Report** for your clients.

#### Key Sections of the Report:
1. **Executive Summary:** Brief summary of the location, size, price, and investment thesis.
2. **Location & Infrastructure Analysis:** Nearby ongoing or planned projects driving growth.
3. **Title & Due Diligence:** The current legal status of the land and verification details.
4. **Financial Projections:** Expected appreciation, ROI, CAGR over 3, 5, and 7 years.
5. **Risk Assessment:** Identified risks and specific mitigation steps.
6. **Recommended Exit Strategy:** Best pathway to realize returns.
